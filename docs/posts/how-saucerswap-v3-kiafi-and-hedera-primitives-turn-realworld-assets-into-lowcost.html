SaucerSwap V1 → V2 → V3: hybrid routing, fee math, and operational hooks

Liquidity models

SaucerSwap supports three liquidity models you must route between when building integrations:

  • V1: classic constant‑product AMM — LPs deposit equal‑value token pairs; pool covers price ratios between assets.
  • V2: concentrated liquidity (Uniswap V3 style) — LPs pick a price range (A → B) to concentrate liquidity near market price; enables single‑sided exposure.
  • V3: club / central limit order book (CLOB) model — accepts signed maker intents off‑chain. Makers sign an order intent that can be canceled without cost; makers pay 0 bps on fill. Takers pay 5–15 bps. If V3 price crosses a V1/V2 pool price, execution routes to the AMM pools for fills.

This hybrid routing creates internal arbitrage opportunities and guaranteed liquidity for fills.

Concrete fee accounting you must implement

  • Smart‑contract AMM swap fee: 0.3% (30 bps) per swap. Allocation: 25 bps to LPs, 5 bps to the platform.
  • Website interface fee (using SaucerSwap web UI): +0.25% (25 bps) on top of the smart‑contract fee.
  • Mobile app interface fee (white‑labeled wallet): 0% interface fee — users pay only the smart‑contract fee (0.3%).
  • V3 maker/taker split: maker = 0 bps on fill; taker = 5–15 bps on execution.

Integration notes for engineers

  • Preserve signed intents offline or in a signed‑intent store and expose a cancel endpoint; do not charge makers until on‑chain execution.
  • Aggregator/routing logic should decide between matching V3 book fills, executing V2 concentrated liquidity swaps, or falling back to V1 pools; monitor spreads and implement internal arbitrage capture if profitable.
  • Track three fee layers in accounting: contract fee (0.3%), optional interface fee (0.25% on web), and maker/taker settlement in V3. Mobile flows should skip interface fee application.
  • Incentives: V2 concentrated liquidity gets “Larry” rewards paid in two‑week epochs; coordinate LP incentive monitoring and distribution with SaucerSwap’s treasury rules.

Hedera primitives: when to use HTS, HCS, HBAR, and Smart Contracts (EVM)

Pick Hedera primitives based on cost, auditability, and programmability. Confirm fee figures via Hedera’s Fee Calculator before production.

HTS — native token issuance and compliance

  • Use HTS to mint fungible or non‑fungible tokens without deploying a contract. HTS supports adjustable supply, freeze/wipe, and account KYC flags.
  • HTS is appropriate for tokenized invoices, tokenized equities (using Asset Tokenization Studio patterns), and assets requiring programmatic administrative operations.
  • Hedera documents fixed USD fees for HTS operations (sample token transfer fees are small; verify exact values in the Fee Calculator).

HCS — ordered messages and settlement records

  • Use HCS when you need verifiable ordering and audit trails (PSP settlement messages, auction order logs, cross‑border coordination).
  • HCS is a notarization layer: record hashes or summaries and rely on the consensus timestamp for ordering and dispute resolution.

HBAR transfers — micro‑settlements and agent economies

  • The call contrasted token transfer cost (~0.1¢) with HBAR transfer cost (~0.01¢), suggesting HBAR is an inexpensive rail for high‑frequency, low‑value transfers between agents.
  • Use HBAR as the settlement leg for extremely low per‑transfer costs (micropayments between AI agents or frequent off‑chain coordination).
  • Always verify HBAR conversion to USD and per‑transaction fees via the Fee Calculator.

Smart Contracts / EVM — programmability and gas capacity

  • Hedera’s Smart Contract Service runs an EVM client and supports Solidity contracts for composability beyond HTS admin features.
  • The ecosystem is testing EVM capacity; the speaker referenced a 150 million gas baseline and an ambitious target ~1 billion gas — treat these as speaker observations and confirm with Hedera engineering docs.
  • For DEXs and high throughput matching, design contracts and off‑chain matching to be modular to take advantage of higher gas ceilings when available.

Vaults, AI agents, automation, and the safety checklist

What vaults do

  • Vaults are smart‑contract strategies that automatically rebalance concentrated liquidity when price moves out of range.
  • Strategies include migrating liquidity to a new range, compounding fees, or rebalancing between single‑sided and balanced positions.

AI agents — opportunities and risks

  • Use cases: predict short‑term range shifts, optimize fee capture, programmatically rebalance vault allocations.
  • Risks: automated strategies may execute at scale and amplify errors or oracle issues. Agentic vaults are experimental.

Safety checklist before deploying agentic vaults

  • Limit exposure: cap TVL per strategy and impose per‑strategy withdrawal limits.
  • Require human overrides: provide multisig or governance emergency pause and manual rollback procedures.
  • Audit and test: obtain third‑party audits and run multi‑month simulations against historical data.
  • Log actions to HCS: use HCS to record agent decisions and rebalances for an ordered, auditable trail.
  • Start with opt‑in governance: require user consent to delegate funds to agentic vaults and be explicit about strategy parameters and risk.
